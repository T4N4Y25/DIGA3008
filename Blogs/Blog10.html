Close reading

A close reading is done on the text, "How Geography Shapes—and Is Shaped by—the Internet" by Shane Greenstein, Chris Forman, and Avi Goldfarb.

The reading aims to explore how geographical locations can affect how the internet is implemented as well as seeing how the internet has affected the economy through three key aspects: communication costs, transportation costs as well as search costs. The reading explores how a geographic location affects how the internet implements these three factors and hence how the internet is more beneficial in some regions compared to others.

The reading uses previous events that the internet had on the economy through these three factors. A "re-structuring" (Greenstein, 2018) of multiple industries occurred due to the introduction of the internet to better utilize the reduction in communication costs, transportation costs and search costs.

Communication costs are said to be significantly lower using the internet as it costs almost nothing to communicate through the internet regardless of the user's location. (Greenstein, 2018).

Transportation costs have been reduced due to the internet using the example of newspapers. In the past people needed to pay to have newspapers and hence local information delivered to them; however, the introduction of the internet has made information widely available to the public free of charge-hence emphasizing how the internet has reduced "distribution costs" (Greenstein, 2018). It is further seen that the use of the internet has allowed users to receive goods without the need to travel to a physical store or location further reducing transportation costs. This reduction in transportation costs is unequal however as more rural locations may lack the proper infrastructure to take advantage of the delivery of goods and information.

Due to the reduction in communication and transportation, economic models have shown that this reduction directly reduces search costs (Greenstein, 2018). With reduced communication and transportation costs, it is easier to make choices as the ability to compare options is enhanced. Similarly, businesses can now more easily hire and outsource work (Greenstein, 2018).

Various models are used in this reading to explore how the internet reduces the importance of certain geographical factors. The first model the reading examines is the "death of distance" model (Greenstein, 2018). This model explores the concept of cities losing relevance as the internet grows due to more reliance on the internet for communication, transportation and searching therefore making the need for "offline channels" more irrelevant (Greenstein, 2018). Geographical location has relevance according to this model as to incorporate the internet more into the economy, a highly developed structure to support this will be required; however, less developed locations will still have a larger reliance on offline channels creating a more unequal divide between the developed locations and the less developed locations. Implementing the internet requires "complementary inputs such as broadband service or expertise in implementing IT systems" (Greenstein, 2018) which less developed locations may lack. Kolko, Moss and Townsend (domain name registrations) show that the availability of the requirements to implement the internet, grows with location size (Greenstein, 2018).

A literature details two views on the internets impact on societies: convergence and divergence (Greenstein, 2018). Convergence suggests that the internet provides more employment opportunities to less populated regions while divergence suggests that the internet only benefits largely populated regions with an already skilled population. Evidence has been provided in the reading supporting both views; however, the evidence provided mostly suggests that divergence is the impact the internet is having on society.

The reading suggests that due to the lower costs of communication due to the internet, innovation can occur more. By making communication more accessible, more collaboration can occur, hence increasing the innovation. According to this quote from the reading, "While invention became more geographically concentrated over this period, this is not true for the counties that were leaders in business Internet adoption", the application of the internet favours convergence regarding the two views on the internet's impact on society (divergence and convergence).

Evidence has been provided on how the application of the internet has led to cheaper goods and more convenience (Greenstein, 2018). The reduction in distribution costs can lead to improvements in less developed locations as well as providing a larger variety of products. The geographic location of a user can however alter which stores and products they view more relevant to their location which can limit the user's choice regarding quality.

"Trade in goods is likely to increase because of lower search and communication costs, while trade in services is likely to increase even more because transportation costs also fall through digital distribution." This quote is supported by relevant evidence and studies and makes sense from a first-glance perspective as businesses will be able to better coordinate their services and will be more widely available to the public through use of the internet.

The reading concludes by confirming that the use of the internet has indeed impacted the economy by reducing costs in communication, transportation and searching. Three more factors further influence the utilization of the internet according to geographic location: "preferences, the availability of substitutes, and the availability of complements." (Greenstein, 2018). Overall, the reading provided sufficient evidence supporting the claims made - regarding the impact of the geographic location on the use of the internet - using models, historical events and data, and relevant examples.

[1] Greenstein, S., Forman, C. and Goldfarb, A. (2018) How Geography Shapes—and Is Shaped by—the Internet. Edited by G.L. Clark et al. Oxford University Press. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1093/oxfordhb/9780198755609.013.21.
