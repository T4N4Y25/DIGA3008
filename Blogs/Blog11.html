Critical Reflection on "Digital inequalities and why they matter" by Robinson

Before I begin the reflection, I would like to point out that all quotes used in this critical reflection come from the text "Digital inequalities and why they matter" by Robinson, 2015.

"The relationship between digital inequalities and other forms of inequality has yet to be fully appreciated" seems to indicate that despite the rapidly growing digital divide caused by the expansion of the internet, the digital divide remains unnoticed compared to other forms of inequality. By opening the text with this statement, the author is giving a brief overview of what the text will entail: the growing concern of digital inequality.

The author continues by describing the traditional forms of inequality: race, class and gender. The author then explains that digital inequality is still relatively new and therefore is going unnoticed despite its rapid growth. Digital inequality is a growing concern as those with more access to the internet and digital services are more likely to succeed as "digital capital play key roles in a range of outcomes, from academic performance to labour market success to entrepreneurship to health services uptake." This point is true when one looks at how rapidly the world is transitioning from an offline community to an online community where most interactions now occur online. Those who do not have access to the internet and digital services are therefore left behind and do not have access to the opportunities that the internet provides. The author then explains that even without the advantages of online capital, "digital inequalities can reinforce existing social inequalities and even exacerbate them because they carry over preexisting differences in human capital into online settings." This is a valid theory as humans as a species tend to fixate on the differences between each other and use these differences as an excuse for prejudice. Historically humans have demonstrated the severity of inequality by creating division according to race, class and gender. These prejudices are in the process of being ruled out of society and steps are being taken to ensure that; however, access to the internet has provided people a worldwide platform to farther spread inequality and prejudice towards others mostly free of consequence (insulting someone in-person can have immediate consequences but doing it online is relatively risk free).

The author further emphasises the unfair advantages that digital capital provides by using the youth as an example. "Institutions demand that youths engage with online resources, youths lacking adequate access are compelled to ration their screen time, often depriving them of opportunities to develop the kinds of valuable web skills which their less constrained peers enjoy." This quote shows a direct consequence of digital divide where institutions expect all children to have access to the digital world. This however can leave those lacking basic access to the internet further behind as they are therefore more excluded academically hence institutes unknowingly prioritise education in those more fortunate in the digital divide. The incorporation of the internet into work life also allows for people to become more easily distracted by content provided by the internet. Therefore, those who are more accustomed to the flow of digital information will be able to function more effectively in a workspace. The elderly are also affected by the digital divide as those who are not well-acquainted with the internet lose out on the opportunity to connect with family and friends remotely.

Gender differences have diminished with the introduction of the internet; however, men do use the internet more frequently than women creating a larger gap in the digital divide between men and women. The author explains by stating that women use the internet for simpler tasks like communication and support, but women also seem to underestimate their online skills. This is interesting as I have personally seen no large differences between men and women in terms of online skills, but this could be due to my environment as I am surrounded by both men and women who demonstrate the same competence of online skills. The author then uses analytics to show the divide in men and women in IT industries – however this could not necessarily be due to digital inequality but rather differing interests as men are seen to use the internet more than women (generally) and this larger interest could promote men to pursue careers in these industries.

Race and ethnicity relating to digital inequality is explored using two hypotheses: stratification (the adoption of the internet "replicates existing social inequalities, as digitally mediated networks replicate offline social network structures and because offline human capital carries over to the online world") and normalization/diversification ("individuals can transform their social networks and social capital by accessing online networks"). Stratification explores the concept that individuals who do not have the resources to access online networks will not be able to benefit from online capital further creating a divide between those with access and those without. Normalization contrasts this by explaining that the introduction of the internet has allowed people to gain access to resources they were not able to access previously.

[1] Robinson, L. et al. (2015) 'Digital inequalities and why they matter', Information, Communication & Society, 18(5), pp. 569–582. Available at: https://doi.org/10.1080/1369118X.2015.1012532.
